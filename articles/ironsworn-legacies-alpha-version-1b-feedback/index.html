Ironsworn Legacies Alpha Version 1b Feedback

This is my response to the questionnaire for the Alpha Version 1b playtest of Ironsworn: Legacies.

These responses were rewritten to be more polite and less jerk-ish. The original response is over here

General Sentiment

I played a sort of two-shot of a single quest. I am not sure if I want to continue that into playing longer. I may wait for the next version, or try playing with someone else first.

I had a bit of a dour time while playing. There was a sense of underlying frustration with the system that eventually just grew into exhaustion and annoyance when I had to engage with it.

Everything is Take Action

The moves of Ironsworn and Starforged have been reduced down into the generic Take Action, with codices helping shape your Take Action for a given context. I am not sure this simplifies as much as advertised. Instead of a flowchart for what move to use, we have one move that takes a flowchart to use.

Further, the actual gameplay of Take Action didn't really change or get spicy either. I received points and went shopping with the points. This could swing as wildly as 1 point to something like 5 points total, but all things, in the end, are advantages and misfortunes to spend on the list. There are no actions that offer more or less risk than any other move or action. The distinction between Gain Ground and Strike cannot exist in Legacies.

While the codices help somewhat, there was definitely fatigue going through the same process over and over no matter what I was actually trying to do in the story. The advantages and misfortune system might be solid for mandating a certain amount of reward or cost for an action, but performing the calculus every time I made a roll got old after a while. It got to a point that I didn't want to roll because I knew I'd be going into the Take Action calculus again.

It was also not clear to me if I was expected to have a purchasing plan in mind when making the roll, or if I was free to just roll then decide what happens once the numbers are in. Some of the codices, mainly about my action taken, suggested that I have an advantage in mind when doing them. Other parts of them suggested unusual or unexpected things.

The Narrative Economy

To quote friend EdgeOfDreams: "It's like having a full combat system for every interaction."

There is a lot of time spent working the numbers. I negotiate with someone for rest and replenishment. Roll. Okay, based off the tallies, I have 2 advantage and 1 misfortune for that. Now I allocate those for the mechanical payoffs. Okay, I have a setback (a setback being a concrete, mechanical item of accounting). To resolve the setback, I must roll again and allocate a point to balancing off the setback. What can I mend? I have exactly these impacts. This one is locked so I can't mend it.

I felt like I was playing less for the story and more trying to keep up with the Narrative Economy. I had to spend points wisely. I had to balance the books.

Position: A Punishment for Being Punished

Position, as-is, serves to punish failures and reward successes. The problem with this is twofold. The first is that I'm already punished for failing and rewarded for succeeding in the form of Misses or Strong Hits and the advantages/misfortunes those give me. The second is that I, the player, have little to no control over what my position actually is. It goes up or down based on the die rolls. If I'm in a bad position and want it better, I need to either score a Strong Hit, or fish for an opportunity (which demands I tempt fate).

It really sucks to pay the costs of a Miss only to be told that for rolling a Miss, I'm going to keep paying costs for the foreseeable future. Likewise, if I'm in a Favored position and getting extra advantages for it, it really doesn't feel like I didn't anything "correctly" to earn the reward.

This system seems designed to cause the failure spirals other feedback reports talk about. Of course a failure spiral is going to happen if one of the results of failure is that anything but unambiguous success gets punished further.

So Much Control, Yet No Control

There is always a sense of the dice deciding how things go in a TTRPG, but with Legacies I especially didn't feel like there was anything for me to "do". I was beholden to the Take Action flowchart and my story felt utterly shackled to the Narrative Economy. In Ironsworn and Starforged discussions there's a common enough mantra that the mechanics shape the narrative. In Legacies, it feels like the mechanics are the narrative.

This is a bit odd because Legacies looks to offer all the control in the world for directing a story how it goes. On the other hand, it doesn't. There is no sense of "easing up" or "ramping up" outside of the Narrative Economy. Your roll demands exactly this many advantages and this many misfortunes. There's no place if I decide I want something different outside of its narrow allowances because the fiction I'm trying to make is so tightly integrated with the character sheet.

Legacy, Themes, and Message

I find myself uncomfortable with the Legacy system and I'm not sure I have the full vocabulary to explain why. I would probably be more comfortable talking about this in a DM than in a public play report test, but the theming and framing of how Legacies is shaping up I am not sure sits well with me.

Miscellaneous Thoughts

The playtest's rulebook says players are expected to start with 3 assets. If that's the case, than no assets in the playtest means we are put into "hard mode" for the test. This is not how players are actually expected to interact with the game, and we are lacking the bonuses we would normally have when starting out.

"Resource" I think is a little too close to "Supply" as far as wording goes even if they're extremely different things. It's Ironsworn and Starforged in me talking, but "acquiring resources" to me would be something related to a Supply track that doesn't exist in this game. There is something about the keywording in the core rulebook that feels off to me in general, but I don't know how to define that more concretely.

I find it strange that everything has been turned into generic challenges except explicitly quests, which also gets subcategories of Quests and Quests You Swore Iron Vows About.

Concluding Takeaways

The overall vibe from Legacies is that, in contrast with how generic it puts itself, there is a highly specific type of story, with a highly specific pacing of highs and lows and consequences and risk, that it wants you to tell. I do not appear to jive with what the system is trying to make me do. I am trying to think of a good word for this, and I suppose "too deliberate" is one. It's ritualized the actions and consequences, and it feels like spontaneity has been discouraged.

It deserves another try, after a revision or two, and possibly with another person. The ground-level asks of the system bothered me enough, though, that it would be hard to want to playtest past those into the longer-term goals of the game.
